01 — Identity

Transformation Agent, not Trainer.

My professional identity is grounded in AI-enabled organisational transformation — operating at the intersection of business strategy, workforce capability, and technology adoption. The Learning Consultant/Solutionist role is not a career pivot; it is the formal recognition of a practice already underway across four industries and twelve years.

The SkillsFuture TSC for Learning Consultant/Solutionist confirms this positioning. Its job role description — "provides consultancy and advisory services on learning solutions... diagnoses workplace performance gaps... drives business results and behaviour change... manages and fosters relationships with stakeholders and vendors" — describes the engagement model I have been operating within LIVO, in partnership with SMF, WSG, e2i, and Singapore's Polytechnics.

The facilitator role sits inside the transformation agent identity — not the other way around. Facilitation is one capability within a broader consultancy practice, deployed at the appropriate stage of the readiness-to-redesign spectrum.

This framing matters because it sets the correct standard against which to measure gaps, strengths, and development priorities. The DDDLP qualification is one stage of formalisation within a larger professional trajectory — not the ceiling of it.

02 — Teaching Orientation

How I facilitate — TPI Profile.

The Teaching Perspectives Inventory (TPI), completed June 2026, provides a structured lens on my facilitation orientation. My subject area is Business Transformation; my learner group is the General Public (working professionals in B2B and enterprise contexts).

Nurturing is my only dominant perspective (38, above the HIT threshold of 36.73). Developmental (34) and Social Reform (35) sit in the mid-zone — above the mean but below dominant. Transmission (29) is clearly recessive — below the LOT threshold of 30.87.

In facilitation practice, this means I design for conditions where learners apply their own judgment, supported by structured prompts and AI-generated drafts. The "you decide" checkpoint embedded in every module of the Applied AI programme is a direct expression of the Nurturing and Developmental orientations — it creates psychological safety around the human judgment moment, rather than positioning AI as the authority.

On the recessive Transmission score

A recessive Transmission score may appear inconsistent with fifteen years of commercial sales leadership. The TPI resolves this: my commercial practice has consistently operated through influence, inquiry, and consultative engagement — not content delivery or expert-positioning. The Dialogue Inquiry / Facilitation-Oriented Practice Profile (FOPP) that emerges from this TPI result is consistent with how I have always led — both commercially and as a facilitator.

03 — Reflective Finding

Actions consistently outrun Beliefs.

Across all five TPI perspectives, my scores follow a consistent staircase: Belief (B = 43) → Intention (I = 60) → Action (A = 66). This is not a gap in one orientation — it is a structural pattern across the full profile. I have been acting well ahead of what I have allowed myself to believe.

"I have been doing AI transformation work for over a decade. My Belief scores suggest I have not yet fully trusted that this counts as expertise. My Actions have always run ahead. The work now is to close that gap — not just in practice, but in how I understand and articulate what I know."

Two specific belief gaps explain this pattern:

Belief Gap 1 — The Map Gap

I have operated at the commercial and customer success layer of AI transformation — diagnosing, scoping, leading, and scaling. I have not yet operated at the architectural layer: independently designing the logical workflows, data models, and no-code automations that underpin what I recommend to clients.

In Computational Thinking terms: my CT Layer 1 (Decomposition) and Layer 2 (Pattern Recognition / Abstraction) are demonstrated through the three-stage spectrum and the checkpoint methodology. CT Layer 3 (Workflow Modelling — encoding the logic into tools and automations) is the honest gap.

Belief Gap 2 — Informed Scepticism About AI at Scale

Fifteen years of watching AI transformation succeed and fail has produced precise, evidence-based scepticism: dirty data, unready organisations, and oversold promises are patterns I recognise early. This is a professional asset — not a deficit.

It is also what built the Readiness Diagnostic into Stage 1 of the practice before anything else. The sceptic and the practitioner are the same person. The low Belief score in this dimension reflects caution, not incapability.

05 — Development Priorities

Three gaps. Three tracks.

Gap 1 — Learning Needs Analysis / CWF 1

I am performing workplace performance diagnosis in practice — the WEO Readiness Diagnostic is direct evidence. What I am formalising is the methodology behind it: making the diagnostic process structured, documented, and repeatable rather than intuitive and PM-dependent. Currently below L4 standard for formal consultancy application.

Gap 2 — Learning Experience Evaluation / CWF 3

My ability to measure outcomes is strongest at the commercial layer — adoption rates, ARR impact, retention metrics. I do not yet have a formal TAE-standard evaluation framework: Kirkpatrick levels, behaviour change evidence, or learning ROI methodology. This is a real gap against the LC/LS TSC at L4.

Gap 3 — Computational Thinking Layer 3 / System Integration

CT Layer 1 (Decomposition) and Layer 2 (Abstraction) are demonstrated through the three-stage spectrum and checkpoint methodology. CT Layer 3 — independently designing and building the logical workflows, grant-rules engines, and no-code automations that operationalise the methodology — is the boundary I am actively working to cross. This is the gap between coordinating an AI engineer and consulting as their peer.

06 — Professional Development Plan

Three tracks. Deliberate sequence.

The sequence is intentional: formalise what works before building tools on top of it. Building workflow automations before the consultancy methodology is documented is how no-code projects fail.

Track 1 · Next 2 months

Consultancy Methodology Formalisation

Taking the decomposition and abstraction already present in practice — the three-stage spectrum, the Readiness Diagnostic, the checkpoint model — and making them explicit, documented, and transferable. Closes Gap 1 (Learning Needs Analysis) and strengthens CWF 1 without waiting for a long-form credential. CT Layers 1 and 2 become formal methodology, not implicit practice.

Track 2 · Next 6 months

No-Code / Low-Code Specialist Capability

Building CT Layer 3: the ability to deliver working POCs, design grant-rules logic, model learning analytics workflows, and scope what is buildable in a client proposal — without full dependency on an AI engineer. Directly addresses Belief Gap 1 (the map gap) and Belief Gap 2 (informed scepticism becomes demonstrable proof in a proposal context). Closes Gap 3 (System Integration / CT Layer 3).

Track 3 · Medium term

Formal Qualification Pathway

DDDLP 2.0 (in progress, IAL) formally closes CWF 2 — Learning Solution Design — and establishes the Learning Mode Design and Learner Profile Analysis competencies at L4. A formal consultancy qualification — GCWPL or equivalent pathway, currently being evaluated — closes CWF 3 and the Redesign layer of the spectrum, enabling independent consultancy across the full readiness-to-redesign spectrum without dependency on the specialist team for the diagnostic and redesign stages. Closes Gap 2 (Learning Experience Evaluation) through the programme's evaluation methodology component.

07 — Career Aspirations

Ten organisations. Five years. A competency map behind it.

The five-year goal — building a portfolio of ten organisations through AI adoption, from readiness assessment through to job redesign — is not an abstract aspiration. It has a structured delivery channel (EWTP/WSG funded pathway through LIVO's partnership with SMF Centre for Corporate Learning), a repeatable engagement model (the three-stage spectrum, currently being formalised as Track 1), and a technical capability roadmap (Tracks 2 and 3) that makes the full spectrum independently deliverable.

The one-year tactical goal is to complete the transition into a formal Learning Consultant / Solutionist role by January–June 2027, with Track 1 and Track 2 complete and DDDLP 2.0 formalised. The ten-company goal is viable when CT Layer 3 is operational — without workflow modelling capability, each engagement remains overly dependent on the AI engineer in the team. Track 2 is therefore not optional for the five-year goal to hold.

The development plan is sequenced so that each track reduces a specific dependency: Track 1 reduces dependency on intuition; Track 2 reduces dependency on the AI engineer; Track 3 reduces dependency on the specialist team for the redesign layer. By the end of the medium-term horizon, the practice is independently deliverable across the full spectrum.

08 — Closing Reflection

The gap is closing. Deliberately.

I have seen AI transformation oversold and underdelivered. I have also seen it work — when the readiness conditions are right, when the job roles are redesigned to meet the technology rather than simply layering it on top, and when the human checkpoints are genuine rather than performative.

My Belief scores reflect the distance between what I have done and what I have allowed myself to claim. My Actions have always run ahead. The B/I/A staircase — B=43, I=60, A=66 — is not a weakness profile. It is the profile of a practitioner who holds himself to a higher standard than his own track record has yet caught up with.

This portfolio, this DDDLP qualification, and this period of deliberate development represent the closing of that gap — in practice, in methodology, in technical capability, and in how I understand and articulate my own expertise. The transformation agent identity is already operating. The formal structure is catching up to it.

"AI drafts. People decide." I design for that principle because I have lived both sides of it. The Readiness Diagnostic exists because I know what happens when you skip it. The checkpoint exists because I know what AI cannot do. That is what this practice is built on — and what the next chapter of it will extend."
